Projects

This project is a prevention program of frailty in older adults living at home. Age-related illnesses create a challenge for individuals, families as well as for social, economic and health care systems. Frailty is a health condition that is defined as a progressive age-related decline that results in reduced reserves of own capacity. Resources in health care need to be concentrated on the older care recipients, a population that is growing in Sweden.

The project aims to enhance and assess a distinctive reablement service called ASSIST 2.0, building upon previous research and feasibility studies. ASSIST 2.0 incorporates insights from reablement research and utilizes technology to improve communication among stakeholders. It involves older adults identifying challenges in daily activities, with homecare staff receiving guidance and education from healthcare professionals to support them. The project will compare ASSIST 2.0 with standard homecare, evaluating its impact on older adults' performance in daily activities, self-efficacy, perceived health, and quality of life. It adheres to evaluation guidelines set by the Medical Research Council and the CONSORT statement for non-pharmacological trials. The central research question is whether older adults receiving ASSIST 2.0 can experience enhanced performance, satisfaction, self-efficacy, perceived health, and quality of life.

Project C aims to explore the potential benefits of technology-based interventions, such as online music programs, digital apps, and virtual reality, for older adults with frailty, including dementia, and their caregivers. While music-based interventions have shown promise in influencing various aspects of health and well-being, including cognition and emotional states, there's a need to bridge analog interventions with technology to create scalable solutions. This project seeks to address this gap by investigating how different technologies can be effectively utilized to improve outcomes for older adults with frailty and dementia. Additionally, the project emphasizes the importance of involving end-users in the development and implementation of these technologies to ensure their suitability and effectiveness in everyday life.

The project intersects across projects "", "" and "", recognizing the critical role of stakeholder engagement on different levels of health intervention design. Drawing on the phrase "all things co-" coined by Pérez Jolles and colleagues (2022), and on ideas behind Patient and Public Involvement (PPI), this project integrates collaborative methods and partnerships to engage stakeholders, including older adults, carers, health professionals, and public policy and management representatives, in designing and refining interventions. By leveraging different types of co-creation methods, the project aims to enhance user experience and improving conditions for implementing the study protocol. It adopts a cascading framework for design, modelling, and production to facilitate engagement across the projects in the research program. Given the ongoing need for understanding effective collaborative strategies in aging research, this project focuses on addressing this knowledge gap. The central research question explores how co-creation methods and PPI can optimize interventions for older adults living with frailty.

Project E aims to characterize the key elements and connections within various interventions related to prevention, reablement, and homecare. It seeks to understand how interventions such as person-centered approaches impact service delivery, workload, user experience, and organizational factors. By evaluating and synthesizing data from Projects A-D, the project aims to develop an implementation model applicable to future primary and homecare settings. The central research questions focus on identifying the components and mechanisms of impact within different services and exploring strategies to facilitate change within the healthcare system and its context.
