“All things co” – Stakeholder engagement in intervention design

Project D cuts across projects A-C and builds on the idea that stakeholder involvement is imperative in designing interventions. Without teasing out historical and nuances terminologies, we borrow from the conceptual thinking, “all things co” and used the term co-creation for consistency. The project integrates co-creation methods to collaborate with stakeholders (i.e., older adults, significant others, care partners relevant in care, healthcare staff, homecare staff, care managers/social workers and management/policy level representatives) to improve user experience, enhance usability, delivery, and implementation strategies. This project will utilize the framework for co-production and prototyping in engaging with stakeholders across the projects outlined in the program. The reason why “All things co” is included as a separate project is because there is still a lack of knowledge about how co-creation can used and what strategies are most useful in addressing needs within intervention research within aging research.

The research question addressed in project D is: How can co-creation methods be used to improve user experience and enhance usability, delivery, and implementation strategies of interventions for older adults living with frailty?

Study design and research setting

Methodologically, the project is grounded in co-creation, which has been shown relevant for implementation in several settings across healthcare. The project will use an innovative combination of visual and qualitative interview methods to generate data together with stakeholders that contributes to identifying priorities, actively work with intervention design, and plan ways to implement. In this program, interventions span from healthcare regulated systems in project A to municipality regulated systems in projects B-C. Therefore, there will be different types of stakeholders that are of relevance and different parts of the interventions will be in focus. For instance, project A will commence with workshops to integrate knowledge/experiences from two separate trials while project C will commence with available interview and process evaluation material from an earlier feasibility study. Outcomes from co-creation can be intervention materials to be used with older adults, education materials to train staff, or delivery methods of an intervention to meet the needs of care partners.

Methods of data collection and analysis

Sampling will be purposive and constitute older adults and all relevant stakeholder groups. Recruitment for each project will be grounded in the unique project needs. Measures will be taken to ensure that relevant characteristics are represented, such as sex, living conditions, and educational level. In the first step, available data from prior projects, review of prior evidence from the literature, and stakeholder consultations will be carried out. In the second step, co-creation methods including a combination of groupwork, interviews, observations, visualisation, and dialogue will be utilised to enhance, redesign, or innovate intervention components that are prioritized. In step 3, intervention components and training materials will be iteratively prototyped. Examples of prototype outputs include: 1) digital learning platforms, 2) learning materials such as slide decks, brief instruction manuals, and pedagogic guides, 3) exercises of relevance for the intervention, and 4) service delivery processes. Data analysis will be conducted in a two-step process and include both thematic and hermeneutic analyses.
